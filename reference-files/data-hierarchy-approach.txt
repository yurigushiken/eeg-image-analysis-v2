The Hierarchy of Your Data
Your data has a nested structure:


Study
 └── 24 Subjects
      └── Each subject has trials for each condition

The question is: At what level do you want to make claims?

Two Fundamental Approaches
Approach 1: Fixed Effects (Pool Everything)

Combine all trials from all subjects → One big analysis → One result
What it claims: "In THIS particular sample of 24 people, the effect exists."

Problem: You can't generalize. Maybe these 24 people are weird. A single unusual subject with many trials can dominate your results.

When used: Rarely in cognitive neuroscience. Sometimes in single-subject clinical cases.

Approach 2: Random Effects (Subject-Level Inference) ✓

For each subject → Compute their effect → Test across subjects
What it claims: "The effect exists IN THE POPULATION from which these 24 subjects were drawn."

Why it works: Each subject contributes equally (one data point). The variance across subjects reflects true individual differences. If you ran the study again with 24 new people, you'd expect similar results.

This is the standard in cognitive neuroscience and what you're doing.

Your Goal: "Show differences in numerical representation depending on condition"
To make this claim generalizable, you need to show:

Not just "my 24 subjects can distinguish 1 from 5"
But "PEOPLE IN GENERAL can distinguish 1 from 5"
Subject-level inference gives you this. When you report p < 0.001 for 1v5, you're saying:

"If we sampled new subjects from this population, we'd expect them to also show above-chance discrimination of 1 vs 5."

The Alternative You're NOT Doing (And Shouldn't)
Some researchers pool trials across subjects:


All 3000 trials combined → Train/test → Report single accuracy
Problems:

No generalization — Can't claim the effect exists beyond these specific people
Unequal contribution — Subject with 200 trials counts more than subject with 50 trials
Pseudoreplication — Trials within a subject aren't independent; treating them as independent inflates your N artificially
Can't compute valid p-values — What's your N? 3000 trials? But they're not independent samples.
